The Lot

1

I imagine most people believe that fortune, good or bad, is rarely constant. That life's circumstances tend to sway like a pendulum, at times bestowing frustration or pain, and other times surprising pleasantries. I believe this too. But for some fortune can swing in the direction of hardship, and continue thus, unrelenting until the life of the sufferer is damaged permanently, or prematurely ended. This I know.

When I was still living in the United States, shortly before moving to Colombia, I read a book which examined in detail the horrors experienced by countless unfortunate souls in the so-called work, and death camps of Germany and Poland. One female survivor recounted how prior to being enslaved at Auschwitz, she would shriek at the sight of a mouse. But after several months in the camp, having grown accustomed to all its evils and degradations, would scarcely react when a large rat ran across her prone body. There were many such recountings in the book as this, others even worse. Each providing ample evidence of how the fortunes of lives can turn bad, and never swing back. Yes, this particular woman survived the camps. But one can only imagine the horrific dreams, and the galling memories that clung to her throughout the remainder of her life. A psyche wrecked beyond the power of time's heal. And of course most in those camps died, their circumstances increasingly grim until the occurance of premature death.

After years of strangely persistent bad luck, only a few nights ago was, I think, the culmination of my personal fortune's downturn. Though I would never equate the breadth and depth of my misfortune to the sufferings by those of Nazis, it is clear to me that for whatever reason - or perhaps for no reason at all - my life was ultimately for failure and hardship. But there were corresponding horrors that night: fat, glaring rats; appalling cockroach swarms; and hordes of humanity's least fortunate too, living among the garbage and the vermin and the sickening smells. Wretched souls of all ages chained to some of human life's lowest depths of filth and degradation. And there was death that night. As swift as a commandant's bullet but even more haphazard, more bizarre. A death not to be erased from my mind, until my mind itself is erased. And because of that awful, freakish death, I was on the run. Why I was led to this low point of confused desperation, from a relatively normal life in the U.S., is a question I have repeatedly pondered. It defied rationality, but yet was real. A dark maze with each path sinking further into hell. To give an idea of the maze's strange descent, I can only detail the journey. Why I was there I do not know.

2

In 2002 my ex wife and I started a restaurant business just south of the western Canadian border, in the sleepy town of Bellingham, Washington. Between us we had extensive restaurant experience, she as a server, book keeper and manager, and myself as a chef. I also had an interest in and appreciation for wine, so we included in our restaurant a small section of bottled wines for our restaurant’s customers, as well as for retail sale.

Oh, the halcyon days. And after a few years working day and night, in which I put all of my culinary skill and youthful stamina to the test, our business was thriving. Hence I was able to hire two very good chefs to take over the rigors of food execution, and begin to focus on what had become my true passion: the wine business.

Wanting to expand the wine business beyond the current space's capabilities, I decided to open another wine store with a larger, higher-end selection, at a different location within the city. And here is where I thought my life had achieved its ideal. With little to start with my ex and I had built a bustling and popular business. A business in which I was able to enjoy the fruits thereof--that is to say the food, wine and fellowship that naturally accompanies a good restaurant--without anymore having to get beaten and dirty with the work. And having a good reputation from the area’s wine buyers, I was pretty sure it was going to be only a matter of time before cash and inventory would be expanding at the new store. Soon, I reasoned, my job would basically consist of weekend trips to Woodinville, Walla Walla, or the Willamette Valley; attending wine and food events (some quite posh); restocking and organizing my new oak bottle shelves; and chatting it up with fellow wine enthusiasts. And with a bit of luck I would also be accounting satisfactory wine sales at each day’s end.

Admittedly not everything was perfect. There were the usual construction drags, unseen expenses and bureaucracy frustrations to deal with before I was able to open the new business. And of late my ex and I seldom liked being together. In fact as time passed, she was coming home at increasingly odd early morning hours, and sometime not coming home at all. I suspected that she was spending more and more time at one or several of the area's casinos (a passion of hers), or that perhaps she had found another guy. Or possibly both--which is what ultimately proved to be the case. But I had passions of my own to pursue, and independent of her. If we were to divorce, that was well within the normal ebb and flow of life’s vagaries. Life changing, of course, but not threatening nor even damaging. We had no kids. Perhaps we would sell the restaurant, split the cash, and I would continue on with the new shop. At some point I would seek another relationship, but no rush with that. My life was good, and my future was promising.

3

It is strange to now reflect that at the time in my life when I most believed things were set to go well, the yet-to-be-told story of my future could be so different. Maybe it shouldn’t seem strange, for life is nothing if not a series of surprises, great and small. When I was in grade school the most popular, best looking kid in class started complaining of headaches. In time he stopped coming to school. I had nearly forgotten of him when, a couple of years later, in a different class at another school, there was a new kid sitting at a table across the room. A short, heavy kid with a puffy white face, beady eyes and a crew cut. My stomach turned as the teacher approached me, grabbed my arm, and made me sit next to him. Suddenly, through the ravages of a near terminal illness, I recognized my former good looking and popular classmate. A boy who, in another time, and for him another universe, had been a good friend of mine. Somehow the teacher knew this, which is why she made me sit next to him.

The initial surprises at this point in my story were neither so sad nor dramatic. But rather a series of revelations and occurrences clearly indicating the trajectory of my life had changed, now inclining downward. One of the good chefs I had hired quit the restaurant, and was replaced by another whose work was poor. While at the new store, I began hearing from long time customers how the restaurant’s quality was declining. Word was getting around and my hard won reputation for good food was deteriorating. And since Bellingham is not a large town, the impact was fairly swift. Business slowed. Different cooks were hired, but none were particularly good and the restaurant’s reputation continued to wane.

Then my ex began entreating me to return, telling me that things at the restaurant were truly collapsing. And though I was fully prepared to sever myself from my ex and our initial business venture, I suspected a meltdown there could very possibly leech any success from my new venture. Also with each passing month of mediocre to bad sales, so declined the restaurant’s value. My ideal life scenario would have to wait. It took a few weeks to find a tenant for the new space, and cost me a month’s rent for breaking the lease. But eventually, taking all the stuff from my new venture and cramming it into the old, I was back in the restaurant.

It was summer, 2008. Since the restaurant had no outdoor seating, nor pleasant view to speak of, business typically slowed during that time of the year. During the present summer a water main also broke beneath the street in front of the restaurant’s front entrance, and for two months the street was under construction with no vehicle and limited pedestrian access. And during summer 2008 the nation wide housing bubble burst causing an economic malaise, greatly exacerbated by continuous repetition of the bad events by an insane American news media. These factors in conjunction with the already sluggish business caused not just a slowdown during summer 2008, but a near stop.

And during summer 2008, after she had been doing extremely well for two years following glioblastoma 4 brain surgery, my mom’s cancer returned.

Though business was all but non-existent during that summer, expenses lived on unabated. In the past my ex had always dealt with the restaurant’s bill paying and finances, since she had the book-keeping experience, and since I was always busy cooking and hustling wine. But now I had the time and felt it necessary to involve myself in the business’s monetary doings. In the summer of 2008 I learned that the restaurant had no cash reserves, was several thousand dollars behind on rent, several thousand dollars behind on state sale's taxes (DOR), owed significant cash to food purveyors, and even owed money to some employees for time previously worked. Summer 2008 I also learned by my ex that the business was behind on payroll taxes as well. When I asked her how much, she calmly told me we owed the IRS about $75.000.00.

She, my ex, was and would be fine. To her the restaurant was always something of a second job, her primary being a decently paid book keeper for a tax consulting firm in town. My whole life was wrapped up in and dependent on our small business. And I just learned that after years of hard labor, the business was on the verge of total collapse.

Immediately I sold off several of my most precious bottles of wine at discounted prices to get caught up with some of the more pressing debt. I took over management and for the remainder of the summer pared down staff, expenses, and inserted myself back into the kitchen. I reasoned that with autumn’s return, business would kick into gear, and I would again be at the helm to make sure things got back on track. In the meantime I looked into the history of the restaurant’s bank activity and learned that my ex was indeed spending a great deal of time, and money, at various casinos in the area. And that the money spent was coming straight from the restaurant.

Though scarcely being able to afford it, I hired an attorney to begin divorce proceedings. To confront and argue with my ex over her unthinkable lack of responsibility was useless. Over the years I had become aware that for her truth was not particularly relevant, and that if she did not wish to explain her behavior, she wouldn’t. And in fact, when I asked her some of the obvious questions, she offered no explanation, refusing to discus her actions at all. After talking with my divorce attorney and showing her the bank statements documenting my ex's restaurant-to-casino cash pipeline, she advised me to offer to take on the business’s debt in exchange for 100% ownership of the business and it’s assets. Knowing that I had about 100k in hard-earned retail wine inventory (which I amassed over the years in lieu of a salary), as well as tangible and business value in the restaurant, it seemed to be good advice. And after speaking to my ex about it, she readily agreed. All of a sudden I was sole owner of the business and ready for the challenge to make it again work. And my ex was free and clear of it’s debt.

But of course I could not predict the persistence of the economic decline.

Fall came but the usual business increase did not. I was having to shave off wine inventory in order to pay bills and keep the business limping along. Nearly every morning upon arriving at the restaurant I would look at the previous day’s sales reports. It was like being sucker punched.

At winter’s onset Mom’s condition had become critical. There had been hope that she would be a candidate for a treatment using irradiated venom from an Israeli scorpion that was proven to be able to release the radiation on isolated cancerous cells, and kill them. But for whatever stupid, bureaucratic reason that fell through. I visited her each week, and each week she was noticeably worse. On December 11, my sister’s Christy’s birthday, she died. Mom, Carole Mae Adams, was a tough, responsible, beautiful person who suffered much and complained little. I hope her reward is great.

2008 was an unusually harsh, snowy winter in the Northwest, and so our normal peak business season was not much different than our non-peak season--which is to say, pathetic. One rare glimmer of light in an otherwise dreary season was a Christmas wine order I received via email for 24 bottles of Cristal. Cristal is a gold cellophane-wrapped champagne, ridiculously overpriced (in my estimation) due, in part, to its favored status among millionaire rappers, and their more affluent lemmings. It was not unusual to receive fat holiday wine orders, but this one, since Cristal at that time retailed for over $300 per bottle, was particularly large. Indeed if all went accordingly, the order stood to profit me close to $2000 of badly needed money. Though I must say it was unusual that the local Cristal wine distributor had, and was willing to sell me, the complete order. Under normal circumstances, because of the wine’s prestige, a relatively small shop like mine could expect to be offered only 2 or 3 bottles per year. Of course during an economic recession certain non-essentials, like $300+ bottles of bubbly, are in slightly less demand. But anyway I got the wine, and the credit card numbers (two were used) breezed through. Afterward the wine was picked up and sent to the buyer’s father without a hitch. The generous (and obviously rich) buyer’s name was Kellis Wood. And his father was going to have a very Merry Christmas.

The ray of good fortune was not for long. The day of Mom’s memorial service was again cold and snowy. I needed to journey about 120 miles south but I had a 4 wheel drive blazer with new all terrain tires, so I wasn’t too worried about the bad weather. The service was to begin at 4 pm. My ex (who had a good relationship with Mom) and I left around 12 noon, giving us sufficient time in the compromised driving conditions. About half way to our destination, my ex needed to make use of a rest stop in Smokey Point, a small town just North of Everett. It was about 1 o‘clock. We were making good time. The blazer was firmly tracking the road and running perfectly. However, when my ex had finished in the restroom, and I attempted to restart the engine, it wouldn’t. It turned over and over and over but wouldn’t fire up. It was near freezing and snowing lightly. We were stuck at a rest stop because of a car that magically would not start. And I’m no mechanic. We decided it would be best to call a cab. It was going to cost a bundle, but I didn’t want to miss the service. We could stay at Mom’s house, which was near where the service was being held, until we figured out how to get back. So I wrote a message explaining the car was stalled, and would be moved soon. I placed the message face up on the dashboard, and we waited.

Because of the crappy weather it took the cab driver almost an hour to arrive. It was a little before 2pm. We had two hours to travel the remaining 60 miles. The driver was a long-haired Native American, odd yet friendly enough, who braked and accelerated his sedan fiercely on the precarious road. A few miles further south, somewhere between Everett and Seattle, traffic slowed, stopped, and crawled. And continued to stop and crawl until we reached the north end of downtown Seattle. There, two city buses had crashed through a guard rail with a portion of each now hanging precariously in air far above the Northbound freeway. Yet another oddity in what should have been a brief trip South. And though there was no apparent carnage, it was clearly irresistible entertainment for the holiday season gawkers, hence the major slowdown. Upon passing the air-suspended buses traffic resumed a normal pace. It was after 4. We would miss the service.

We decided to go straight to Mom’s house. After paying the $200 cab fare we entered just as the post memorial service guests were arriving for the remembrance dinner. It began snowing heavily. In order to make arrangements to have the car moved to a repair garage, I called a tow company in the Smokey Point area. I knew it was going to be another hefty expense but it seemed the best thing to do. I was told by a man from the company that because of snow the trucks were currently all in use, but that he would call when one was available. He would get from me all the needed information then. After waiting a couple hours and not receiving a call from him, I called him back. He told me he had tried calling several times but couldn’t get through, and that now all repair shops in the area were closed, so he couldn’t move the vehicle. And since the following day was Sunday, and all the shops would be closed, he couldn’t move it then either. There was nothing wrong with my phone. There was evidently nothing wrong with his phone. But still he couldn’t get through. Odd.

I used Mom’s car, a 2007 Subaru Forester, to drive back home the following morning. To see if by some miracle my car would start, I stopped at the Smokey Point rest stop along the way. My blazer was gone. I called the local tow company to see if they had the car, and was told they did not, and that the county police probably had it towed. I was given a number to call. Indeed, though sitting for less than 24 hours at a nearly unused rest stop, and with a note explaining it would be moved soon, some jerk of a Snohomish County police officer thought it worth his time to have my car towed--and probably in the middle of a snow storm. Perhaps my dead Blazer was a threat to the Smokey Point community at large. There was a sizable towing expense that I would need to pay to retrieve my errant car, as well as a daily fee at the lot where the car was taken. And of course it being Sunday, the lot was closed.

I took a few days off between Christmas and New Year’s eve, staying at the beautiful Willow’s Lodge in the heart of Woodinville Wine Country, one of my favorite places on earth. Yet another expense I really couldn’t afford, but I needed a break from the relentless shit-blizzard I was constantly plodding through . And of course to get drunk. Cristal

14

Over the last several months my wine shelves displayed increasingly less bottles due to the long business lag. Now I was prevented from buying any replacement wine at all. And in order to make the wine shelves look at least respectable, I was forced to pull out bottles from my “cellar”-- a small temperature-controlled wine storage room. These were wines destined to improve with time and therefore, in theory anyway, could be sold hence at a higher price. They were wines purchased for long term keeping in order to give depth to my wine business, as well as for my own future enjoyment. An investment process begun several years past, now necessary to begin to undo. Removing these bottles to sell before their maturity wasn’t torture. But it made me sad to have to do it. My “golden tomorrows” were being systematically erased.

Probably because of the long spell of nasty weather, and because many people had been confined for so long, when the weather improved toward the end of January, we had a solid spike in business. My uncle titled the email, ARE YOU IN THE NEWS????, and in the text he said that while watching a news program at his Washington State home, there was a story about how unusually heavy rains were causing big problems for Colombians living in areas Northwest of Bogota.... El Penol happens to be Northwest of Bogota. I guess I was in the news.

I hired Gillermo and a couple other locals to remove the fallen dirt, to dig out the back earthen wall at an angle, and to plant grass there--thus ensuring an accident of this kind would not happen again. And a couple weeks later the house, hence called "Cabanita", was again erected. I knew of course, that in a way the heavy rains and house’s destruction was a blessing, for if not occurring when it did, it clearly could have happened with the house completely finished, and with people inside: a far greater tragedy. With the lesser of the tragedies having occurred, though, the amount of money I had remaining was becoming uncomfortably short.

I really did not wish to run out of cash in a foreign country. Julieta’s clothing business took in some money, but probably barely enough to even cover the housing and utility expenses of our apartment in La Floresta. And I did not want to subsist, day in and day out, on rice and arepa--the cheap, local corn cake staple. But I reasoned that Medellin being a large, vibrant city, someone with as much culinary experience such as I had could probably find a restaurant job quickly enough. And if not, I imagined we could at least sell the lot, possibly for substantially more now that it had a house (albeit without plumbing or electricity) and gazebo. And then perhaps look for another cheap lot to buy, fix up or build on, and sell. It could be an interesting new business.

28

I was wrong about being able to find work. What I learned was that though Medellin, and especially the Poblado district, verily teems with restaurants of all types and quality levels, the labor pool of locals is abundant and cheap, thus to hire un Americano is rarely considered. It mattered not if I insisted I didn’t need to be highly paid. The unwavering belief among business owners was that whatever talent I possessed could be matched by some local, and at less cost. So why bother to hire a gringo.

Not having any success finding employment I decided to make some improvements on the house and lot and put the property up for sale. There is a popular internet site in Colombia called “Mercado Libre,” similar to “Craigslist” which I used to post a for sale ad with price, description and photos of the house and land. Via telephone and email, responses started coming in fairly quickly. A great majority of the responses, however, were from Colombians clearly with a penchant to drink or dream excessively, offering ridiculous trade proposals, or comically low cash offers. Another block of the responses contained more reasonable offers, such as requests to make payments, or car + cash trades. To the latter offers I often responded expressing interest, but any follow up from those initiating the proposed business transaction were rare. It was strange and frustrating. I was learning a hard lesson about doing business with a certain type of Colombian man: his words are mere words. I was receiving near daily inquiries and offers from Colombian caballeros who en masse seemed to have no intention of completing a deal for the property.

Even with those Colombianos serious enough to make the 2 hour drive to see the lot, the trip often evolved into absurdity. One morning, for example, we received a call from a man named Florentino who wanted to go to the property a little later that day. Julieta and I agreed to meet the man at his house, and afterwards go with him in his car to the lot. (We did not have a car.) We arrived at Florentino’s house as agreed, and hung around there chatting with his family and extended family, until finally after an hour or so, it was decided we should leave. So we left. With Florentino (who looked like a small Robert De Niro), his family and extended family. It was a three car, two motorcycle caravan. Each motorcycle had two riders, and all three cars were packed. The journey began about 1 o’clock. Along the way Florentino, who was seated in the passenger seat in front of me, offered me a bottle of Mountain Dew. Though I dislike soda, I accepted his offer, and took an unwanted gulp of the beverage. Nope, not Mountain Dew. But rather Aguardiente: the hard, licorice-spiked swill preferred by the locals. I smiled at Florentino as the warm, sweet-burning liquid slithered down my unsuspecting throat. Thanking Florentino profusely, I handed him back the “Mountain Dew,” explaining it was perhaps too early for me to appreciate.

29

Upon reaching the town of El Penol it was decided--as it was nearly always decided by Colombians--to stop for food and drink. It was then about 2:30. It took quite some time to get everyone--there were 18 of us--assembled and seated into the chosen humble restaurant. It took some time to get the food ordered. It took a lot of time for the no doubt surprised and harried cook to simultaneously prepare food for 18 people. Towards the end of the meal, at about 5 o’clock, I told Julieta that we really needed to get going. But of course it took some time to pay and get everyone reassembled into, and onto, the vehicles. At about 6 o’clock, as the Antioquian sun began its west-horizon descent, we reached the dirt road entrance leading to the property.

About halfway along the road to the lot, the three cars, none having good road clearance and each ridiculously burdened with an excessive passanger load, were unable to pass a series of potholes. I jogged the next mile or so behind the two motorcycles to the top of the lot, but by then it was dark. It was a clear night and there were many lovely stars to behold above. But the lot below was an amorphous black void. Not a particularly good showing.

Returning to the rest of the clan, there was little to report on the lot’s virtues. And after a dithering, Chaplin-esque process of turning the three overloaded cars around--then stopping at a food stand for arepa and chorizos--we drove back to Medellin. Heading home I now accepted the proffered Aguardiente from Florentino, who had not altered his clockwork sipping since--indeed most likely before--we left that day, around 1 o‘clock. Sometime after midnight Julieta and I were back in our apartment. It was December 26th. We never heard again from Florentino who, perhaps only through the licorice glow of Aguardiente, thought it would be nice to own a lake front lot.

There were other weird, useless trips to the lot. Often with a carload of family members, and always for a meal or snack along the way. Upon arriving at the lot, sometimes the inquirers were seemingly unimpressed, at other times clearly approving. But the end was always the same: no sale. I began to believe that for many of the Colombian families, it was really more of an opportunity for a fun mountain sojourn, than a serious investigation to buy land. In many cases Julieta and I both suspected that the “interested” inquirers didn’t even have money. But why should that get in the way of one’s curiosity and a pleasant day‘s jaunt? Then one day we got a phone call from a Colombian with the unlikely name of Anderson Smith who seemed clearly different.

30

Anderson Smith was a thick, charcoal-hued fellow from Choco, the western Deep-Jungle portion of Colombia. He drove a fairly new silver Toyota sedan, and evidently owned a successful land excavation company. With only he, Julieta and I in the car--a first!--we drove--directly!-- to the lot. He loved it. And after an hour or so enjoying the fresh air, spectacular scenery and sunshine, Anderson Smith made an offer. It was a few million pesos less than the asking price, and was to be made in two installments, half at the week’s end and the other half within two months. But the offer was reasonable enough, so I accepted. Stopping only briefly for a beverage, we then returned to Medellin. And en route we made arrangements to meet at his bank on Friday. But....

Friday came and Anderson was in Choco on business. He wouldn’t be able to meet until the following Tuesday or Wednesday, and would call then. On the phone he assured us of his complete commitment to buy the lot. After Wednesday passed with no word from Anderson, we called him the following day. We told him we were getting other inquiries for the property. He again insisted he was going to buy, but that he needed us to bring the property’s papers to his attorney, for her to look over. And as soon as she gave the nod, he would have the cash.

The soonest we could arrange a meeting with his attorney was the following week. We made copies of the property’s documents, and dropped them off at the attorney’s office on Monday. After unsuccessfully trying to reach Anderson the next few days, we finally got a hold of him on Thursday. He said that his attorney told him the papers were all in order, but that he would be busy until the following Tuesday. And that yes, yes! he was going to buy the property. Though Julieta still believed the deal with Anderson was destined to close, I had my doubts. I was once again telling other inquirers the lot was available, but since all the inquiries were from Colombianos, they were dubious at best. Tuesday came, and surprisingly Anderson called. We again made arrangements to meet, first at his bank for the cash on Thursday at 10 am, then on to a notary for the sale/purchase agreement. I was starting to breath easier. It looked like the sale would be made before I had completely run out of money. Thursday at 9 am we were set to go and meet Anderson when the phone rang. Julieta answered the call. I could tell it was bad news. Yes indeed, it was a woman from Anderson’s business office who said he was on a job site, and could not meet. Appalling. Throughout the weekend we tried to reach Anderson, but he wasn’t answering his cell phone--at least not when we called. Finally on the following Tuesday Anderson Smith answered our call. He wasn’t interested in the lot.

31

An American living in Colombia once wrote that concerning a certain Colombian man, words are mere words. After a few months of dealing with this admittedly generalized man--in the relatively simple context of trying to sell him a small tract of lake property with an unfinished house--I was bitter, frustrated, and worst of all, broke. For a while longer Julieta and I lived in pleasant La Floresta off her modest business sales, and SOS money one of my sisters was generously depositing into my U.S. bank account. But soon our expenses, though not excessive, were exceeding our means. And we had to move. And that, of course, meant relocating to Julieta’s house in Bello.

Along the North/South electric rail line, which parallels the latte-brown and fetid Medellin river, are numerous Metro stations spilling out into diverse Antioquian districts. Generally along the North/South line, those areas further south--areas such as Poblado, Aguacatala and Envigado--are cleaner and safer, with higher income homes, better shops and malls, and a greater police presence. On the Northern side, especially from the Caribe stop (where the North terminal bus station is) to points further North, is where one can witness some real third-world squalor. Bello is the next to last stop on the north end of the metro line. Bello does not have the profound poverty--the long stretches of rotting wood-and-metal shacks, the mountains of stinking trash--as some of the previous stops have. Places such as (the aforementioned) Caribe, and Tricentanario, and Acevedo. But Bello is certainly rougher, drunker, grittier, hillier, busier, noisier and edgier than much of Antioquia.

About 2 miles from the metro station, up a steep mountain side covered with the burnt-orange brick homes common in Antioquian residential areas, is Julieta’s abode. It’s not really a bad place, but humble. A two story section of a brick and concrete building with a simple kitchen, back open air patio, and steep, concrete stairs connecting the two levels. Upstairs are three small bedrooms, two in the house’s front and the other in back. From the two front bedrooms the view is quite spectacular, overseeing the busy and populous Bello valley, and outlying Andes mountains.

But I didn’t like living there. There was only one small bathroom shared by Julieta, her two younger kids, and I; and though the house was kept clean, there were cockroaches. I was also resenting more and more the idleness, the inability to earn an income, the apparent impossibility of being able to sell the lot, and our semi-impoverished circumstances. And having virtually no money, save that which my sister periodically sent, there was little I could do to change things. Until that damn lot sold, I was stuck.

Bello House

32

Julieta worked day and night fashioning pretty and colorful bras and panties to sell in Bello and the nearby town of Copacabana. Her son Andres kept busy with his job at the water purifying plant, with friends, or on his motorcycle. Sarah, Julieta's daughter, spent days at the University of Medellin with her film studies, and nights at home frequently clicking photos of her large, tiger-striped cat "Tony" for some school project. Sarah was never without her digital camera. But I had little else to do than brood over my again crappy circumstances. I worked to keep in shape taking long walks around the hilly area, doing push ups and sit ups, and regularly pumping a set of Andres’ leaden arm dumbbells. But I was bored and broke. It appeared that what I previously had thought was a change in my ill fortune, was really only a respite: that bad luck had hounded me to the Southern Hemisphere.

One day when I was in a particularly dark mood I started an argument with Julieta over some trivial thing that I was disgruntled about. Even with the lousy circumstances I didn’t often argue with Julieta, for I knew full well that none of my problems were due to her. And that she in fact was doing her absolute most to make things as pleasant for me as possible. She was a paradigm of optimism, patience, industry, and honesty. I loved and admired her deeply. But on that day I needed to vent. The argument began and escalated. For a while we even shouted at each other, something I can’t recall happening before. And to make matters worse, after things cooled down I found out that Sarah was home in her upstairs bedroom, she not feeling up to going to school that day. She no doubt heard it all. When I regained my sanity, I felt like an ass. I later apologized profusely to Julieta, who understandably stayed thoroughly piss off at me.

The next day, though, Julieta’s anger was spent, and between us things returned to normal. Julieta worked most of that day in the downstairs part of the house where her tables and sewing machines were. And as evening approached, she decided to go to our bedroom upstairs to rest a bit before dinner. The two kids were away, both having left very early to their endeavors. From fresh tomatoes--which like so much of the fruit and vegetables in Bello were sold cheap on every other street corner--I started a sauce to be used with pasta and hamburger. It would be a typically humble dinner but good. I had become a bit of an expert in scrounging around the numerous food stands and markets in the area for the best deals available. It was a slightly different routine than battling the crowds at Costco, as I used to do each week when shopping for the restaurant. Slightly different indeed but with the common thread of finding food on the cheap. My mood was better that day, and I was glad Julieta had forgiven my previous unpleasantness. I was also anxious to have Sarah know that the nasty shouting she heard yesterday was nothing serious.

33

With the tomato sauce simmering, I started my evening workout: 30 push ups and 30 sit ups to be followed by 40 simultaneous arm curls with the dumbbells. I had already taken a walk that day, and was hungry, as I knew Julieta would be also. Dinner was getting close. I wished that it was possible to get some bread other than the sweet, airy crap ubiquitous throughout Colombia. I heard some muted rustling from the bedroom upstairs, and in a moment Julieta appeared at the top of the stairs. She was watching me pumping the dumbbells and smiling her beautiful, radiant smile. “Huele muy rico, amor,” she said, commenting on the sauce’s delicious, garlic and tomato aroma. And then she walked down the stairs. Or at least that’s what should have happened.

What actually happened was that after a couple of steps she inadvertently stomped on one of the legs of Sarah’s cat, which was sleeping on the staircase. The cat screeched, turned about and viciously bit into Julieta’s bare ankle. Startled and in sudden pain Julieta reactively jumped up kicking her foot away from the cat. Losing all balance she hurled forwards on the steep staircase. I heard a sickening crack as the side of her head slammed into the corner of one of the bare concrete steps. She tumbled onto the floor and briefly convulsed, as though in an epileptic seizure. A bulbous, gashed welt quickly formed on her right temple. Stunned I went to her. The beautiful honey color had vanished from her face which had turned gray-white. I dropped the dumbbell from my left hand, and took her left wrist, but could feel no pulse. I felt for a pulse on the left side of her neck, but there was none there either. The right quarter of her limp-hanging head was now grotesquely swollen. Then Sarah walked in.

We were in plain site of her. I was bent over Julieta with my left hand clasped on her neck. The right side of her ashen face had clearly been struck by a hard blow. And in my right hand was the other leaden dumbbell.

Sarah’s eyes and mouth were stretched open in utter disbelief. And though it was not clear to me why, perhaps out of sheer habit, she reached in her pocket for her camera, and took a picture. Then started screaming. Of course I knew what she thought. I dropped the dumbbell, and raised my hands. Walking towards her I tried to tell her that Julieta fell, that it was an accident. But looking at me in complete horror and loathing, she ran out the front door. Her loud screams, now interspersed with sobbing, piercing the neighborhood.

34

I had literally no time to think things through. It was quite possible--quite probable--the joy of my life had just been killed in a freak accident, and most certainly I would be judged as the cause: I would be deemed my wife's murderer. The night before there was the argument. Sarah would surely condemn me. The photo would provide proof. It would seem so obvious. I would be hastily tried in a hostile Colombian court, and then....I knew not what. Executed? Thrown into a horrific Colombian prison...? My God....Julieta you can't be dead. But looking at her I was sure. That growing, sickening welt. And her head so limp, lifeless. She was dead. My beautiful Paisa. My lovely soul mate. Mi otra mitad.

I grabbed a coat and my backpack and fled. I was sure Sarah would return soon with the Police, a lynchmob, or both. What the hell was I going to do? I was running down the precipitous streets. I needed to move. Get out of Bello. Get out of Colombia. But how? I would be pursued.... Hunted. And God help me if captured.

Down, further down I ran. I crossed the busy central park. I was nearing the Bello Metro station. But I knew I couldn't use it. There, perhaps, they would be waiting for me. It was almost dark. I needed to hide. Turning South I hopped a chain-link fence and crossed a series of old train tracks. I reached the Medellin river. The filthy Medellin river. Continuing south along the east side of the river I moved on. I needed to get to the bus terminal at the Caribe station. I had 12 thousand pesos cash, which would get me to El Penol. There at an ATM I would withdraw what I had remaining from my U.S. bank account. Thank God I had grabbed my back pack with my wallet inside. There was a bus stop a couple blocks away from the terminal where I could catch the ride to Penol. In the terminal they would surely be looking for me. But I would not go inside. And try and move only in the shadows. If I could just get on a bus for El Penol, then I could get to the lot. My beautiful lakeside lot. I could think things through there. But first I needed to get to the Caribe terminal. Acevedo, Tricentenario then Caribe. Oh shit. I had to pass through that festering third world slum I had seen countless times traveling by on the Metro. How many times I imagined the horror stories that surely must be common there. And soon I'd be in that evil place. And at night. There simply was no other option. None.

Medellin River

35

The moon was out that night, about 3/4 full , and as the occasional cloud passed beneath it the Medellin river displayed many different facets of its brown, stinking ugliness. I was hustling through Madera, the sparsely populated stretch of green hills and new condos between Bello and Acevedo. How dramatically the landscape would soon change. I could make out some of the frightful little hovels ahead. I could smell the vast tracts of garbage along the river. Closer and closer my nightmare approached. Then I was inside.

It was among the worst areas in Medellin, a city of over 3 million people that has many bad areas. Most of the city's slums are strung out along the mountains above. Miles and miles of hastily thrown together wood, metal, and brick structures, some precariously pitched on mere sticks against the steep hillside. Ironically a majority of these dirt poor hillside abodes have million dollar views of the vast Andes mountains, and the sprawling Medellin metropolis below. Many of these slum-residents are families that were, in years past, forced off their rural mountain land when the infamous Colombian violencia was in full fury. Apparently Pablo Escobar, one of the 20th century’s richest coke dealers, paid to have these shanties built in order to garner support from the masses that would occupy them. And indeed to many of Colombia’s poor he was, and yet is, a hero. But intense poverty has always been the hallmark of these Medellin hillside areas--poverty matched by violence. Know a tree by its fruits. Gang wars, torture, assassinations over drugs and weapons are common. By and large the Police stay away.

Taking a narrow road that hugged fairly close to the river and Metro track, I hastily made my way through the slum. The squalor was worse here than were I to take a route higher up the hillside, but I sensed it would be safer--that the worst of the criminal elements would likely steer clear of the lower filth. I walked by row after row of foul, haphazardly stacked brick structures and dumpy wooden shacks. Some of the sordid residences had impossibly large families crowded around the tiny rag-strewn yards and porches, or mashed together on rotting furniture, staring at old television sets. In front of many of these appalling homes older women were cooking sancocho, a type of Colombian stew, in old metal pots over small fire pits. The gray, billowing smoke from these fires had a foul, toxic smell. There were lean, sickly, rash-infested dogs that roamed and sniffed about on nearly every street. I was sure that here rabies was common.

After a mile or so the road sloped downwards, closer to the river. Impossible as it seemed, the living conditions became worse. This was a veritable trash dump with heaps of putrid garbage piled along the river bank, and people--families--in dilapidated tarp and wood boxes actually living among the filth. Garbage dwellers.

36

The stench of rot, sewage and river-dumped chemicals was almost tangible. I struggled to keep from vomiting. I was incessantly harassed by large, buzzing insects. Occasionally one of the garbage dwellers would notice me, and the hostile recognition of an outsider, an alien, would gleam maliciously from his eye.

From my peripheral vision I caught frequent shadowy glimpses of what I first thought were more of the sickly dogs, but soon realized were rats. And as I trekked along the rats came closer, moving more slowly, more brazenly. Unlike the dogs, these animals were fat and healthy, with vicious red eyes--clearly thriving in the festering squalor. I picked up the pace, was nearly moving at a jog. Ahead in the moonlight I could make out the body of an animal lying in the gutter. As I approached I saw that it was indeed the decomposing corpse of a cat, and that a swarm of huge cockroaches were busy feasting on what little remained of its flesh.

More garbage, more stench, more rats, bugs and roaches. More horrific poverty as I continued south along the river. The road arched up and away from the Tricentenario Metro station, then wound back towards the viscous brown river. The small, wood-and-plastic structures here had no electricity, no lights. But from the moonlight I could often make out slum-befouled human activity. Filthy children playing in piles of debris, laughing as any child in a U.S. suburb; small, deformed men and women, with face-boils and rotted teeth, languishing atop improvised seats of moldy cardboard or piled stones. And yet again, bent, elderly women boiling something--I tried to refrain from thinking what--in old tin pots over reeking, trash-made fires.

When I owned my restaurant/wine business I regularly attended food and wine tasting events hosted by the numerous wine distributors in the Seattle area. Among the swankiest of these was an annual event that took place 70+ stories high, at the top of the Columbia Tower in downtown Seattle. It was sheer hedonistic excess. Banquet tables heaping with trays of brightly colored melons, pineapples, strawberries, grapes; rich blue and green veined, triple-cream cheeses; great slabs of beef, ham, turkey and salmon; sausages, pates and aspics; huge baskets of fresh-baked breads; pastries, cheesecakes, tortas, chocolates. And of course wine. Table after table of some of the planets most delicious nectars: Napa Valley Cabernet; Washington State Syrah; Barolo and Barbaresco; Burgundian Chardonnay; German Riesling; Oregon Pinot Noir; Bordeaux; Chateauneuf du Pape; Vintage Port. Literally hundreds of different wines to sample from lovely crystalline stemware beneath sparkling chandeliers. And in the company of scores of formally attired men and women fortunate enough to have been born with such incredible opportunity for pleasure. I couldn’t help thinking of some these events of my former life, as I snaked through this reeking third world sewer. Nor how utterly different could the lives of human beings be simply due to chance.

Tricentenario hillside

37

Continuing on through Tricentenario the road again twisted steeply upwards to the left, and into a barrio less squalid. Here was one of Colombia’s rough hillside neighborhoods. And I well knew this far more dangerous than what I had just gone through. Dim streets with music blaring from laundry-heaped, dirty brick houses; youths openly smoking pot, or doing coke; recklessly ridden motorcycles roaring by; grim, booze-quaffing men beneath scarcely lit, shoddy cafe awnings. I kept my head down and hurried along. As in the garbage-filled slum of Acevedo, there was no police presence here. Nor, like the Acevedo slum, would I need to worry about the police coming here to find me. Only a suicidal americano would enter this world, where the lives of Colombians have only scant value; and of Grinos, no value at all. One could only image how many human corpses had become the putrid feast of vermin along the trash-strewn riverbed below.

Further along I approached a group of tattooed thugs gathered in front of a small, iron-bar enclosed liquor store. They were standing around a small table with a two-liter bottle of Aguardiente on it. I smelled pot smoke. One of them caught my lowered eye, and I sensed he recognized me for what I was: a lost, frightened foreigner. A man woefully out of his element. He said something to the others and they all laughed. Then said something--I think to me--that I was unable to understand. I kept walking.

“Oiga tonto, tu no debes ser aqui,” he continued, and they again laughed. This I understood: “Hey dumb ass, you shouldn’t be here.” Holding my breath, I walked on.

“Alguien se va a cortar tu garganta y robar tu dinero,” he mocked. “Someone is going to cut your throat and take your money.”

“No tengo, no tengo,” I said, the strained tone of my voice utterly betraying fear.

The gang erupted in laughter. “Gringo pendejo, corre, corre.”

My legs were trembling and uncertain, but I yet continued walking. Perhaps because of my humble attire--which I made a point to wear in Colombia--they believed I really had no money, as I had said. Or who knows, maybe the pot had mellowed them--or maybe they just weren’t the thieving, murdering type. But they didn’t follow me. At length my legs steadied and I hustled along, praying to hence be unnoticed. Eventually the road sloped downwards, and I was again passing through the dark slums near the river. Finally the lights of the Caribe station bus terminal came into sight.

38

I wasn’t certain how far, nor for how long I had been walking. Nor was I certain of the time. But at last I came upon the bridge leading to the Caribe bus terminal. I crossed the bridge and took the stairs down to the street, avoiding going inside. Trying as best as I could to stay away from any lights, I got to a point close to where the bus for El Penol would be stopping early the next morning. I found a tree-darkened area and sat down. And in spite of the recent nightmare fresh in my mind, I dozed off.

I caught the 7 am bus, and a couple hours later I arrived in El Penol. I was weary and confused. The surrealistic past events and abruptness in which my life and future had horribly twisted, rendered the world a strange, menacing, evil place. I was grief-stricken, alone and fighting hard not to cry.

While in El Penol I thought about using an internet cafe to try and reach my sister. If made aware of my circumstances, she would certainly transfer enough money into my account for me to fly back home. But I knew I couldn't leave the country. By the time all my travel affairs were in order, Medellin airport security surely would be on the watch for me--if they were not already. Perhaps they even now had the damning photo. I would be apprehended and jailed. Likely abused, and tortured; maybe then executed. On my life the iron door had shut; the illness pronounced terminal. I literally had no hope. And I realized that all my previous problems, no matter how bad they seemed, were really trifles. For in the past I always had at least some option to make a change, to alter my life’s course, and perhaps realize a happier tomorrow.

At the lot at least I would have a little time to reflect unmolested; and the opportunity to control my near-future’s grim choice. I went to an ATM and withdrew the extent of my cash: 40 thousand pesos, about 20 buck’s worth. I shopped at a couple of places nearby, filling my backpack with prepared food and a 5 liter bag of water. Then I bought a pen and notebook. At 10 o’clock I took the transport to the stop near my property, and walked down to my house and kiosko by the lake.

Of course I knew that Sarah would lead the Bello Police to the lot, once they had unsuccessfully searched the city. Sarah knew I was broke and thus limited in options. She would conclude, as had I, that the lot would be the best place to go. The amount of time I would have before the Police's arrival I also knew would be short, a couple of days, or perhaps only a few hours. So I began at once on my story--the story I am now writing.

39

I thought at first about only writing of Julieta's tragic, accidental death as a potential advocate for my defense, if I choose to be apprehended. For of course that is one of my two choices. But as fate would have it there has been sufficient time to write from the start of my life's decline. It has been interesting to recall it again, pondering over many of the details. How odd and ironic it has been.

With the time I have been permitted here, I have often been tempted to succumb to perfect bitterness, and to “curse God” as Job was tempted. And though sick with confusion and resentment, like Job I have refrained. All earthen life is short, all earthen pleasures fleeting. This reason knows, in spite of emotion’s objections. Barring instant death each of us must acknowledge the inevitability of suffering and termination. And present among life’s possibilities for everyone is the path of ever worsening circumstances.

Perhaps I shall at some point know the reason for, and hence understand, my life’s descent: The failure of my business; the loss of my modest wealth; the abridgment of my liberty; the sudden death of my wife. And at last the hard choice.

Though I am yet in good health amid a world of sunlight, color, and motion here at this beautiful place--my lot, if only for a moment longer--this too must soon end. And indeed, now they come. During moments of clarity I have seen that this life is but a sadness. Why is a mystery. But we all, at length, must accept the lot we have been given.
